Inverter vs Converter — Direct Comparison
Understanding the fundamental difference helps with troubleshooting, upgrades, and system sizing.
Converter / Battery Charger
AC → DC
Primary Function: Converts shore power or generator AC into DC to charge batteries and run 12V DC loads.

When Active: Whenever connected to shore power or running a generator.

Key Characteristics:
• Multi-stage smart charging (Bulk / Absorption / Float)
• Powers all 12V systems (lights, pump, furnace, controls)
• Usually always on when plugged in
• Lower idle power draw than inverters

Common Issues: Overheating, fan noise, insufficient charging for large battery banks or lithium upgrades.
Power Inverter
DC → AC
Primary Function: Converts battery DC power into 120V AC to run household appliances when off-grid.

When Active: Only when you need AC power without shore power or generator.

Key Characteristics:
• Pure sine wave (recommended) or modified sine wave
• Must handle surge loads (microwave, AC compressor, well pump)
• Idle power consumption is a major battery drain
• Size for both continuous watts and surge watts

Common Issues: Low battery voltage shutdown, overheating on sustained high loads, modified sine wave interference with electronics.
Recommendation for Most RVers
Upgrade PathReplace old converter with a modern multi-stage charger when adding lithium batteries.
Inverter ChoicePure sine wave inverter sized for your largest expected load + 20–30% headroom. Consider combined inverter/charger.
MonitoringAdd a battery monitor (BMV, Victron SmartShunt, etc.) to see real-time usage and state of charge.
Typical RV Power Scenarios
How power flows in common situations.
Scenario 1: Connected to Shore Power
Shore power → Converter/Charger → Battery charging + all 12V DC loads.
AC loads (microwave, TV, etc.) powered directly from shore power via transfer switch.
Inverter is typically in standby or off to save battery.
Scenario 2: Boondocking / Dry Camping (No Hookups)
Solar + Charge Controller → Battery Bank.
Battery → DC loads directly.
Battery → Inverter → AC loads as needed (use sparingly to preserve battery).
Monitor state of charge closely.
Scenario 3: Generator Running
Generator → Automatic Transfer Switch → Powers AC loads and Converter.
Converter charges batteries while generator runs.
Good strategy: Run generator periodically to top off batteries and run high-draw AC appliances.
Scenario 4: Combined Inverter/Charger Unit
Modern all-in-one units (Victron MultiPlus, Magnum, etc.) handle both directions intelligently.
When shore power is present: acts as charger + passes through AC.
When shore power is lost: automatically becomes inverter.
Often includes built-in transfer switch and advanced battery management.
