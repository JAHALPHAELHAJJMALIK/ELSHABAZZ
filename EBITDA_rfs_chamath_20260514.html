EBITDA explained through the lens of Robert F. Smith and Chamath Palihapitiya

RFS

Robert F. Smith

Founder & CEO, Vista Equity Partners · PE lens

THE CORE FRAMING "EBITDA tells you how much cash the business is capable of generating from its core operations — before anyone takes their cut. It's the number that tells you whether the underlying engine actually works."

What it strips out & why

Interest expenseFinancing decision — not operations
TaxesJurisdiction artifact — varies by deal structure
DepreciationNon-cash — prior capex already spent
AmortizationNon-cash — accounting write-down of intangibles
THE PE DEAL PERSPECTIVE RFS uses EBITDA as the denominator in deal multiples. A software business bought at 12× EBITDA means: if EBITDA stays flat, you'd recover the purchase price in ~12 years. His Vista playbook focuses on expanding that EBITDA — operational efficiency, pricing power, churn reduction — to make the exit multiple work.

THE CAUTION RFS also warns: EBITDA is a proxy, not the truth. High EBITDA with high capex needs (heavy industry, hardware) is very different from high EBITDA with low capex (pure software). Always check EBITDA minus capex — that's true free cash flow power.
CP

Chamath Palihapitiya

Founder, Social Capital · VC/SPAC lens

THE CORE FRAMING "EBITDA is the Rosetta Stone of finance. It's how you compare a German industrial company, a Brazilian retailer, and a San Francisco SaaS startup on the same axis. It normalizes away the noise of capital structure and accounting choices."

The "noise" Chamath wants to remove

Different debt levelsInterest varies → distorts net income
Different tax regimesCountry/structure artifact, not ops
Different asset basesD&A timing is an accounting convention
One-time chargesOften adjusted out in "Adjusted EBITDA"
THE VC/GROWTH TAKE For high-growth companies, Chamath argues EBITDA margin trajectory matters more than the current number. A company going from −40% to −10% EBITDA margin in 3 years is showing operational leverage — the ability to scale revenue faster than costs. That's the story VCs pay for.

Margin expansion — the real signal

THE CHAMATH CRITIQUE He's also a prominent critic of EBITDA abuse. "Adjusted EBITDA" can strip out stock-based comp, restructuring charges, and more — turning a cash-burning company into a 'profitable' one on paper. His rule: if a company's adjustments exceed 20–25% of EBITDA, be very skeptical.
