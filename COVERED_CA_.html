What is Covered California?

Covered California is the state's health insurance marketplace, established under the Affordable Care Act. It helps individuals and small businesses find affordable health coverage.

2024 Silver Plan Updates

For Californians earning between 200%-250% of the federal poverty level, there are significant cost reductions in the Silver plan for 2024, including $0 in-patient hospital stay and lower costs for primary care visits, therapy, and generic drugs.

Program Eligibility

Eligibility for Covered California programs depends on your income relative to the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). Various plans and financial assistance options are available based on your FPL percentage.

Individual Mandate Status

While the federal individual mandate penalty was set to $0 in 2019, California has implemented its own state-level mandate. Californians must have qualifying health insurance or face a state penalty, unless they qualify for an exemption.

RMB: Required Minimum Benefits

The Affordable Care Act (ACA), also known as Obamacare, requires health insurance plans to cover certain essential health benefits. Here are the 10 required minimum benefits:

1. Ambulatory patient services (outpatient care)
2. Emergency services
3. Hospitalization
4. Maternity and newborn care
5. Mental health and substance use disorder services, including behavioral health treatment
6. Prescription drugs
7. Rehabilitative and habilitative services and devices
8. Laboratory services
9. Preventive and wellness services and chronic disease management
10. Pediatric services, including oral and vision care

These essential health benefits must be included in all individual and small group health insurance plans sold both on and off the Health Insurance Marketplace. Large group health plans and self-insured plans are not required to offer all of these benefits, but most do. It's worth noting that while these are the minimum required benefits, many plans offer additional coverage beyond these essentials. The specific details of coverage can vary between plans and states.

How to Get Covered

To get covered:

  1. Explore available plans on the Covered California website (https://www.coveredca.com)
  2. Select a plan that's right for you
  3. Enroll during the open enrollment period (typically November to January) or during a special enrollment period if you qualify
  4. Start using your coverage for preventive care and other health needs

Open enrollment for 2024 coverage runs from November 1, 2023, to January 31, 2024.

Origins and History of the Affordable Care Act (Obamacare)

2008

Barack Obama campaigns on health care reform as a major issue.

March 23, 2010

President Obama signs the Affordable Care Act into law.

2011-2013

Early provisions of the ACA are implemented, including allowing young adults to stay on parents' insurance until age 26.

October 1, 2013

Health Insurance Marketplaces, including Covered California, open for enrollment.

January 1, 2014

Major provisions of the ACA take effect, including individual mandate and prohibition on denying coverage due to pre-existing conditions.

December 2017

Tax Cuts and Jobs Act passes, setting the federal individual mandate penalty to $0 effective 2019.

January 1, 2019

Federal individual mandate penalty reduced to $0. Some states, including California, implement their own individual mandates.

2020-Present

Ongoing implementation and modifications to the ACA, including responses to the COVID-19 pandemic and enhanced subsidies under the American Rescue Plan.

ACA Evolution and COVID-19 Impact

The ACA has evolved significantly since its implementation:

  • Elimination of the federal individual mandate penalty
  • Implementation of state-level individual mandates in some states
  • Expansion of subsidies and eligibility under the American Rescue Plan
  • COVID-19 related changes, including special enrollment periods and coverage for testing and vaccination

These changes reflect ongoing efforts to improve healthcare access and affordability in California and across the nation.
